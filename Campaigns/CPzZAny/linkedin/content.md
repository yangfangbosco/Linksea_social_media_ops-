# When enterprise AI capabilities are already strong, the real question is: can visitors experience them through one coherent interaction?

At the Tencent Binhai Exhibition Hall, the challenge was not to add another standalone showcase.

The client already had its own large model capabilities and 3D digital human resources. The real task was to bring those assets together through a single interaction entry point—one that could make the technology feel connected, visible, and easy to engage with.

That is where the AI digital human became more than a screen-based guide.

It became the unified interface of the exhibition space.

By integrating the client’s existing AI stack and combining it with multimodal perception capabilities such as facial recognition and lip-synced wake-up, the project turned a static showroom into an interactive environment that can respond, explain, and guide more naturally.

More importantly, the digital human works in coordination with the hall’s multimedia content, helping present the company’s development journey, technical strengths, and business landscape in a way that feels less like a sequence of displays—and more like a continuous conversation.

What makes this case interesting is the design logic behind it:

- not adding one more tech feature, but unifying fragmented capabilities
- not making interaction more futuristic at the cost of usability, but making it more natural
- not building a one-time installation, but creating an intelligent carrier that can keep evolving with future content

For enterprise exhibition spaces, this shift matters.

As companies accumulate more AI capabilities, the competitive difference is no longer just how much technology is on display. It is whether that technology can be organized into one convincing experience that people can immediately understand and engage with.

That is the deeper value of an AI digital human in a showroom setting:
not just as a presenter, but as the front-end of enterprise intelligence.
